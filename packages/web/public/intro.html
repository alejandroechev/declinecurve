What is Decline Curve Analysis?

Decline Curve Analysis (DCA) is the most widely used method in the petroleum industry for forecasting future production from oil and gas wells. Every producing well experiences a decline in flow rate over time as reservoir pressure depletes, and DCA captures this behavior mathematically.

The approach is empirical ‚Äî it fits mathematical curves to observed production history without requiring detailed knowledge of reservoir properties. This contrasts with physics-based reservoir simulation, which models fluid flow through porous media but requires extensive data and computation. DCA trades physical rigor for practicality: an engineer can produce a credible forecast in minutes with just a production history.

DCA matters because it underpins reserves estimation. Proved reserves ‚Äî the quantity of petroleum that can be economically recovered ‚Äî are booked on corporate balance sheets and directly affect company valuations. Regulatory bodies (SEC, SPE-PRMS) accept decline curve analysis as a valid method for estimating proved developed reserves.

Arps Decline Equations

J.J. Arps published his foundational decline equations in 1945. The family of curves is governed by a single parameter b (the decline exponent) that controls the curvature of the production decline.

Exponential Decline (b = 0)

The simplest model. Production declines at a constant percentage rate ‚Äî the same fraction of remaining production is lost each time period. Common in boundary-dominated flow (depletion drive reservoirs).

The decline rate D is constant throughout the well's life. A straight line on a semi-log plot (log rate vs. time) confirms exponential decline.

Hyperbolic Decline (0 < b < 1)

A more general model where the decline rate itself decreases over time. This is the most common behavior observed in real wells, particularly those with water drive, gas-cap expansion, or other pressure support mechanisms.

The b parameter controls curvature. Higher b values produce a gentler decline (slower loss of production rate). The decline rate decreases from Di at time zero and approaches zero as time ‚Üí ‚àû.

Harmonic Decline (b = 1)

A special case of hyperbolic decline where the decline rate decreases linearly with cumulative production. This is the most optimistic of the three models ‚Äî production never reaches zero, declining ever more slowly.

Harmonic decline is rarely the best fit for an entire well life but may describe certain periods of production, particularly in wells with strong water drive or gas recycling.

What b-Values Mean

  • b = 0 ‚Äî Constant decline rate. Common in depletion-drive oil reservoirs in boundary-dominated flow.
  • b = 0.3‚Äì0.5 ‚Äî Moderate curvature. Typical of solution-gas drive reservoirs.
  • b = 0.5‚Äì0.8 ‚Äî More gradual decline. Often seen in gas wells or reservoirs with partial pressure support.
  • b = 1.0 ‚Äî Harmonic decline. Theoretical limit; rarely observed over a full well life.
  • b > 1 ‚Äî Sometimes seen in early transient flow (tight/shale wells) but is not physically sustainable long-term. Forecasts using b > 1 require a terminal decline rate switch.

Estimated Ultimate Recovery (EUR)

EUR is the total volume of oil or gas a well will produce over its economic life. It is computed by integrating the decline curve from the start of decline to the economic limit.

EUR drives reserves booking: proved developed producing (PDP) reserves are directly estimated by this calculation. Companies use EUR to plan field development, evaluate acquisitions, and report to regulators.

Model Selection

DeclineCurve fits both exponential and hyperbolic models to your production data and selects the best one automatically. Two statistical metrics guide the choice:

R¬≤ (Coefficient of Determination) ‚Äî Measures how well the model explains the variance in the observed data. Values closer to 1.0 indicate a better fit. An R¬≤ > 0.95 is typical for good decline data.

AIC (Akaike Information Criterion) ‚Äî Balances goodness-of-fit against model complexity. A lower AIC is better. This penalizes the hyperbolic model for having an extra parameter (b), ensuring the simpler exponential model is preferred when both fit equally well.

When to Use Each Model

  • Exponential ‚Äî Mature wells in boundary-dominated flow with a clear constant-percentage decline. Semi-log plot shows a straight line.
  • Hyperbolic ‚Äî Wells with pressure support, early-life decline, or unconventional (tight/shale) production. Rate-time plot shows concave-up curvature.
  • Harmonic ‚Äî Rarely used as a primary model; consider only when data strongly supports b ‚âà 1, typically in strong water-drive reservoirs.

Production Forecasting

Once a decline model is fitted, it can be extrapolated forward to forecast future production. DeclineCurve generates forecasts for 12, 24, or 60 months (configurable).

The forecast produces a monthly rate schedule and cumulative production at each time step. An economic cutoff rate can be applied to determine when the well becomes uneconomic. Below this rate, the forecast terminates and the total cumulative production equals the well's EUR.

Forecasts are shown as dashed lines on the production chart, clearly distinguishing historical data from projected values. Both the rate and cumulative curves are available for export.

Practical Considerations

Data Quality

  • Use consistent time intervals (monthly is standard). Irregular data may need normalization.
  • Exclude shut-in periods (zero production months due to maintenance) or account for them in the time axis.
  • Filter out early transient production (first 3‚Äì6 months in new wells) before fitting.
  • Ensure rates represent stable decline, not artificial fluctuations from operational changes (choke adjustments, stimulation jobs).

Rate-Time vs. Rate-Cumulative Plots

The rate vs. time plot is the standard view, but rate vs. cumulative production can reveal flow regime changes more clearly. On a rate-cum plot, exponential decline appears as a straight line, while hyperbolic decline is concave-up.

Operational Events

Workover operations, restimulation (refrac), artificial lift installation, or infill drilling can alter the decline trend. When these occur, the production history should be segmented, with separate decline fits before and after the event.

Glossary

EUR (Estimated Ultimate Recovery)
Total cumulative production expected over the economic life of a well.
qi (Initial Rate)
Production rate at the start of the decline period, typically the peak rate.
Di (Initial Decline Rate)
Instantaneous fractional decline rate at time zero, expressed per unit time (e.g., /month).
b-factor (Decline Exponent)
Dimensionless parameter that defines the shape of the decline curve. Ranges from 0 (exponential) to 1 (harmonic).
Decline Rate
The fractional rate at which production decreases per unit time. May be constant (exponential) or time-varying (hyperbolic/harmonic).
Cumulative Production (Np)
Total volume of oil or gas produced from a well up to a given time, obtained by integrating the rate curve.
Economic Limit
The minimum production rate at which operating revenue equals operating costs. Below this rate, the well is uneconomic.
AIC (Akaike Information Criterion)
A statistical measure that balances model fit quality against complexity. Lower AIC indicates a better model.
R¬≤ (Coefficient of Determination)
Statistical measure of how well the fitted model explains the observed data variance. 1.0 = perfect fit.
